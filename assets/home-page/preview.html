Bright Green Field (2021)
Squid
Review by themilkeyedmender
One of the best books I read last year was Christopher Priest’s 1977 novel A Dream of Wessex. The Britain of the book’s reality-sphere is a dystopian, cold, grey island subject to some sort of military occupation, a totally humdrum and dismal place. But the novel’s central characters, a team of academic researchers, have created a kind of collective dreaming apparatus, a VR machine visualising a potential future. The Britain of the simulated future is totally different -- lush and languid, all windsurfing and unhurried lunches at seafood cafés. Once inside, the participants have no memory of their previous lives, and often form relationships with people inside the simulation who they wouldn’t have given a second glance to in real life. Towards the end of the novel, the ontological categories separating the worlds start to collapse; fume-belching power stations of the other world suddenly blight the creamy pastures, the dreamers start disappearing out of thin air.

Squid’s debut album reminds me of Priest’s novel in a number of ways. These eleven songs serve as a journey through a strange pastoral landscape, a Wessex of the unconscious, where everything grey and dismal about England today (and England sub specie aeternitatis) is refracted into a lush, bewildered Other England, latent paranoia and anxiety transmogrified into playful, chewy aphorisms. Patient and in control! Dig holes like a mole! Pamphlets through my door, and pamphlets on my floor! Ollie Judge’s delivery of these lines smack of David Byrne in all his energetic, almost camp, playfulness. The high tempos and krauty interlocking rhythms throughout this album are indebted to Talking Heads and their school of thought, something which sets Squid apart from their South London Scene(™) contemporaries, who tend to draw more from the more emotionally fraught pallettes of noise rock and post-rock. No, Squid are arch and cryptic, emotionally mysterious, dispersed into a pastoral dream.

That’s the second time I’ve used the word pastoral. Maybe I’m being deceived by the album title and cover, but to me Bright Green Field feels like the latest in a line of artistic works wrestling with and revising English pastoralism, with the most notable predecessor in that regard being Richard Dawson’s 2017 masterpiece Peasant, where the medieval Northumbrian countryside became a land of alien presence, strange technology, shapeshifting travellers and druids. Documentary Filmmaker exemplifies what I’m talking about here -- warbling synths interact with soft trumpets and clean guitar figures, placidly, as Judge sets the scene, cryptically as always, describing a documentary filmmaker prowling a hospital ward. Then suddenly the tempo is fucked, the trumpets are tooting, and Judge is screaming about the weather like a man possessed. But the cleanness of the guitars, and the surreal tone of the synth, keep things abstract and green; the hotness of the summer and the coolness of the winter serve as encrypted stand-ins for something else, pastoral metaphors for what cannot be expressed directly (or perhaps what loses its fascinating power if expressed too directly). As the song winds back down to a defeated slump, the documentary filmmaker sneaks back into the frame; he was filming all along.

When I say Squid are indebted to the Talking Heads school of post-punk on this album, I don’t mean to say that they lack originality. Mark Fisher’s powerful litmus test for pastiche and nostalgia -- is this post-punk as a typographical font, a set of established signifiers being faithfully paraded around in the right order? -- is easily passed on this album. Musical weirdness and strangeness constantly frays the edges of this music; Peel Street concludes with a counterintuitive diminuendo, an eerie dissolving of tension into blankness and nothingness, while Boy Racers boasts a surreal raga-esque synth-drone plus vocoder interlude. The relentlessness of the high-speed krautrock rhythms deserves credit, too -- sometimes the album reminds me of the bright post-punk revivalism of much boilerplate 00s UK indie music, except Squid speed it up, repeat it more, intensify it. There’s a wonderful unpredictability to this music, the characteristic perhaps most similar to the band’s South London contemporaries. Even as the syncopated rhythms circle round and round, it feels like each one could suddenly smash-cut to frenzied whiplash, a new scene, a different plot on the coordinates.

I’d like to end with a paean to Narrator, a song I somehow managed to resist listening to until I got a copy of the album. It embodies everything great about this album. It starts in full Talking Heads mode, funky and silly -- here “I am my own narrator” feels like postmodernism in its playful mode, a positive swapping of masks and signifiers. But then in the second half of the song, stormclouds gather, and “I play mine” takes on the tone of a solemn mantra, a subject trapped in the locked dreamworld of Wessex, acutely aware of the masks on top of masks, masks going all the way down. As Martha Skye Murphy fully steals the show with hair-raising, unhinged screams, Wessex collapses back into grey England. Something is off with the perspective on the album cover, isn’t it? The hills seem too smooth, the horizon too far away and infinite. It just doesn’t seem quite real.

Bottomless Pit (2016)
Death Grips
Review by COSMIC_CADET
It's kind of funny. I figured this would be the band's darkest effort yet from each sparse bit of information drip-fed to us on the run-up to its release -- and really, can you blame me? First there was the stark, uncompromising title: Bottomless Pit, revealed by way of late actress Karen Black reading dialogue from Zach Hill's (presumably cancelled) film script; then came the baffling, erratic Hot Head single; the bizarre, sneering cover artwork; and finally the digital and physical release dates paired with, most crucially, an early reveal of every single lyric from the album.

Just one skim-read through Stefan Burnett’s latest slew of meta maniacal abstraction at the time conjured up enough grotesque, obscure and barbaric visions to rival the complete works of J. G. Ballard. The epically defiant war-torn imagery of Spikes; Ring a Bell's savage belittling of its shallow call girl “mosquito”; the insubmissive abandon of Eh; BB Poison’s self-referential piss-takery. Believe me, I could go on and on. However, the sound of the album that houses these themes and stories paints a very different picture to the one formed by the lyrics alone. It's a cackling portrait of the band not giving a care in the world what you expect from their art; a euphoric freak pop trip through anthemic explorations of the band-fanbase relationship, bordering on a kind of twisted self parody previously untouched upon in their impressive discography.

Death Grips continues to toy with the idea of a release cycle -- nay, the concept of what it means to be a band in the digital age in general -- while still delivering an exceptional set of songs with what can broadly be described as their second pop album. Fair be it for modern music’s most intriguing (and arguably, most important) band to unleash an LP like this when everyone -- or at least I -- expected the complete opposite.

The descriptor of “pop” is to be taken with a pinch of salt in relation to Bottomless Pit, but to delve into its importance to these songs is crucial in understanding just how brilliant they are. With tracks as unhinged as Giving Bad People Good Ideas and Hot Head, you’re probably not going to turn the heads of your average top-40 consuming party-goer -- at least not without a beguiled look of grimace on their face -- but that’s hardly the point.

Some have described the sound of the LP as a perfection of the art of abrasive ear candy, something that I’d wholly agree with on the off-chance that I’m willing to indulge in a little hyperbole. But Bottomless Pit is abrasive; and indeed, Bottomless Pit is pure ear-crack… and while this may be the first Death Grips album in a while to not dish up something completely out of left-field, the results come insanely close to being as astonishing as anything we could have possibly hoped for.

If it’s tunes you want, the album delivers in spades. Opener Giving Bad People Good Ideas weaves a sticky and sultry Clementine Creevy vocal in and out of Ride’s vivacious, demonic speed-babble. Together they frame the album as one set to delve into the elusive band’s BDSM-style relationship with their varied cadre of listeners, from the irritating 4chan memers who claim to enjoy the band’s music strictly on an ironic level (pfft), to the self-assured serious fans such as myself. Ride also takes a moment to berate the “senile kings” whose deluded vision of the contemporary music industry depicts “shallow plots” -- a true "fuck yeah" moment. The same industry that the band pissed in the face of being chewed up by and spat out of back in 2012 also comes under rapturous fire. All of this lyrical framework is delivered over accelerated blast beat drumming from Hill and loopy, menacing synth melodies, complimented with a hammering hardcore punk fight riff courtesy of frequent collaborator Nick Reinhart who returns from the Jenny Death sessions. It’s hard to imagine a more exhilarating, uncompromising opener for the album. Blistering and grimy as it is, the mixing is crystal and powerful, a quality that continues throughout the album.

Lead single Hot Head receives a bit of a makeover and it’s all the better for it. The drums sound more dynamic, the mix in general has improved and the new musical additions -- an oddly funky bassline during the chorus and a lugubrious synthetic intro -- add some much needed flavour to the song, upgrading it from a somewhat baffling curio to one of the band's best and most maniacal recordings.

Spikes is, for lack of a better description, catchy as all hell, with a hook that not only rivals Death Grips' entire back catalog but also crushes practically every other pop song I heard in 2016 in terms of sheer virality. Frankly, I’m not sure the infectiously stuttered chant of “Spikes! Spikes! Spikes! Spikes! Spikes!” and its accompanying guitar riffs will ever leave my head. The instrumental here is the finest on the entire record, packing a delightful combination of deranged bustling beats and epic synth stabs.

The low-key Eh delivers some of the record's most shining lyrical moments over bubbly girder synth melodies and proves that Death Grips are a much more dynamic entity than their nay-sayers might initially deduce. Ride mocks those same nay-sayers on the lumbering glitch hop anthem Bubbles Buried in the Jungle, a chant-worthy fixture of every live performance the band have played since the release of the album.

Deep cut 80808 demonstrates Ride's ability as an MC, with its subtly shifting flows recalling those found on my favourite No Love Deep Web cut, Lock Your Doors. This track is another that sees Ride acknowledge the outside world, essentially serving as a diss to anyone who dares attempt to replicate his newly dubbed priceless touch (“Cop my steeze, make yours much fresher... or whatever,” he shrugs gracefully). and Three Bedrooms in a Good Neighbourhood? Practically every section of this song is uncompromisingly viral, even if I still can't figure out its lyrics after months of having the album on repeat.

The title track closes the record with an earned victory lap that speaks directly to the band’s fans. With its addictive and melodic crisp guitar tones sweeping around Zach Hill’s clattering drums, it’s not too far from feeling like the triumphant Yang to On GP’s suicidal Yin. “I’ll fuck you in half. I fucked you in half.” Stefan's gloat of “Death classic, bitch” is earned. The song, and in turn the entire lyrical side of the album reaches its apex in the libretto leading up to Ride's immensely satisfying roar of “This pit's bottomless!!” If you ever wanted to hear what a happy Death Grips song sounds like, give this a spin.

Granted, there are a few pitfalls to be found here, but they’re ultimately few and far between. I appreciate its slow, sludgy structure, but Warping feels a tad too familiar; and while the incessant repetition found throughout Trash serves a good purpose when it comes to the song's mission statement, its instrumental certainly could’ve used a bit more deviation.

BB Poison suffers from a similar case of over-familiarity when you take its skirmish of time signature changes out of the equation. Even then, this track contains enough brazen synth loops and fourth-wall breaking lyrical hoodoo to make it something of a highlight. The aforementioned Warping is the only track that has prompted a skip from me after a rather ridiculous number of listens to this album, and I say that with complete confidence in light of consuming Bottomless Pit’s contents more-so than any other album released in 2016.

I make no secret of The Money Store being my favourite Death Grips album to date and while their latest lacks the initial shock of hearing the band's approach to pop sensibilities and structures for the first time, Bottomless Pit in many ways feels like the culmination of everything they’ve learnt throughout their career being siphoned into what in some reality may have been tentatively titled TMS2.

Death Grips’ future is as uncertain as ever. Whether or not they’re done with recording music at this point is anyone’s guess. But one certainty holds weight, perhaps even as much heft as the group’s latest lacing of thick synthetic beats, heavy drums, roaring guitars and blaring vocals: as the new year approaches, Bottomless Pit continues to make the wait for more Death Grips music positively effortless.

9

Maps (2023)
Billy Woods & Kenny Segal
Review by Hurricanslash
I don't like being in transitional spaces. Hot take, I know, but the feeling of not knowing how long a certain journey will still take, feeling like things are taking longer than they should and not being able to really get to where you want to is... absolutely uncomfortable to the nth degree for me personally. I mean, it shouldn't bother me that much, the journey is often much more important than the destination, but still, something is holding you back somehow and you don't quite know how to deal or reconcile with it. It's a feeling of being quite on edge.

Maps is a giant transitional space for Billy and Kenny. Envisioned more or less like a travel log of thoughts and experiences from tours and trips and you know, this album seems like a big collection of vignettes at first. Kenny is giving Billy some surprisingly normal yet intensely detailed and textured jazzy production to hop on, and then he does just that, in his usual fashion. Yet, something is different here, no? Billy is in conversational mode on here, way more than he usually is, and it is, indeed, about the uncomfortable sensations around transitional spaces. As he weaves together his usual snappy bars, he is putting himself way more out in the open, quite in the vein of Church, but as that album looked inwards, Maps looks more outside of that. It's an album about questioning yourself, asking yourself what your humanity even means to other people, how much expectations can poison your mind, how you reflect upon change in yourself while asking if this is where you wanted to go. How much your home feels like your home after you've been gone for so long, especially if the next trip is right ahead of you, the airport gates you spent hours sitting in watching other people going through the same transitional spaces as you. You all want to get there, but you are not there yet.

This is not a negative album, or a positive one despite the more optimistic slant to it overall. There is a destination in sight, and you are going to reach it somehow, but not just yet, not right now. Maps is the personification of ruminating about everything while having way too much time to spend in spaces you are only in for a short amount of time, really going through it all. It's also one of their most instantly accessible albums, so if you want a skeleton key to a lot of Billys lyricism and Kennys style of production, this is for you. Just... very touching and conversational all around.

American Football [LP4] (2026)
American Football
Review by SinsOfArcadia
[05.01.2026]

I think they may have created their defining album, and not in the way that LP1 is often considered.

The drumming is outrageously creative, and the guitar melodies play off of one another in a way they've never quite explored, while the bass serves as a sort of mediator that has quite a lot to say on its own throughout the record.

I can confidently say that this is their most comprehensive album, and also their saddest (?). Maybe that's just me really feeling these lyrics and the soothing plight of the instrumentation, I'm not quite sure yet. There is also a pretty extensive use of non-traditional instrumentation throughout, and much of that does remind me of the debut.

I genuinely thought LP3 would forever be their second-best album, but there's no doubt in my mind that this has supplanted it.

To even feel that way, if I'm really being honest with myself, seems to cheapen it, as LP1 was made so long ago, and has become such a staple in Emo's storied canon in a way that feels like it should stand alone and separate from what came after it.

To that end, I would say - somewhat reluctantly - that this is their peak. To be transparent, I haven't really had this record on my radar, so I approached it in a way that wasn't quite apathetic, but certainly void of any major expectations.

To my surprise and delight, this record is absolutely packed with thoughtful, experimental songwriting and manages to lock in a desperate sense of melancholy.

With that said, there's a sort of definitive reality setting in as I find myself plucking words from the ether after the album comes to a close. That is to say, I feel confident enough to genuinely apply that description to their entire output.

Considering the fact that I've been along for the entire ride, from care free teenager to middle aged adult who finds himself in his fourth or fifth major existential crisis, I guess the greatest thing I can say about this is not really a statement of quality, technical prowess, or lyrical sensitivity at all.

I'm much more inclined to give thanks to this group of individuals, if not just for their music, but for the guidance and connection I have felt over the years, as each of their albums have been there for me at some point or another while I really needed to hear them, and almost always in a different light due to the way time seems to change things that sit perfectly still.

As a sort of stinging contrast to that, I cannot in good faith state the same about almost anyone in my life, with a few major exceptions.

Because of those exceptions and the kinship I've felt with these albums, the best thing I can say about any of it is honestly reserved for my own inner dialogue, and maybe a gentle smile - the ones we used to make when we felt the fluttering in our stomachs and the beating of our hearts.

Cosmogramma (2010)
Flying Lotus
Review by dutchfool
The first 10 seconds you won't know what hit you. Maybe you've heard his other albums before this, but nothing'll prepare you for those 10 seconds. After, this album dives headfirst into a sound that is otherworldly, ethereal and beautiful, yet immanent, powerful and infectious. The harps and the bleeps paired with some harsh, fast-paced drums'll tell you that in about 1 minute time. In the next 3 you'll be able to explore this new world of Flying Lotus a bit, but never does it give you any space to breathe: there is much to hear on this album and it won't wait for you to catch up. The bass is the fuel that drives the track: notes that seems so fit they can't be improvised yet so frequent and playful they couldn't be written.

Nose Art will tell you though, that there's only one boss in this world: Steve Ellison. An odd catchiness make this a powerful track; it could be Hip-Hop, could be Dubstep, could be anything. Whatever it is, it's very in your face for a track with a sample as big and ethereal as that one; though you might mistake it for a synth. Lotus acts cunning here: he hides samples where you don't expect them, yet you always know they're there, wherever they are. He's too much of a knob twiddler. After that, it almost seems like the album gives you some time to catch up, with an intermission that plays as an introduction. It introduces one important factor for this album that has not been apparent before: emotion. before you know it though, Cosmogramma throws you right back into its wilderness. As if you have gotten a taste of the world's problems and then get a new look. That's the greatness that is Zodiac S**t: it does what the first tracks have done, but gives you some more air to breathe, while again being filled with bleeps, bass, synths and strings.

Computer Face does exactly what the title tells you: it shows you how important the electronics are for this album (lots of people call this an electronic album, but it's like calling Radiohead's In Rainbows a guitar album; yes, it's there but that description just doesn't suffice at all). The track shows how much Flying Lotus has mastered laptop music and let's you know how his tracks are stone-cold killers; he moved much further into new territory than his idols ever did. Steve definitely owes some to his musical compadres (who have executed perfect musicianship; names include: Thundercat, Miguel Atwood-Ferguson, Ravi Coltrane, Dorian Concept, Rebekah Raff), but it's his mastermind at work throughout this whole album. These first couple of tracks could almost make you forget in what kind of situation this album was made.

Flying Lotus has said it often in interviews: this album has been severely affected by the death of his mother (and also the echoes of his late much-missed great-aunt Alice Coltrane - it should be clear). It shows in the track with Thom Yorke (though Thom Yorke seems to be nothing more than a puppet to Flying Lotus' knobs). Suddenly, the album shows you shards of the emotion that lie on the depths of it, never giving you the time to fully understand. The metaphysical longing is very much there, but it can't show you more. The track topples over from the strength of that emotion and results into a very strong jazz track that is as free yet unsettling as you'd expect. At the end strings end the song an a more comfortable note and it immediately plunges you into a very calm track, as if the pace of the first half of the album never happened. Suddenly you have the time to soak it all up and feel the beauty of this cosmic world. Thundercat assists with a very simple yet very effective message: just be who you are. It would've been corny if it wasn't for the bizarre yet super-effective composition of the track.

Suddenly the album feels comfortable and Do The Astral Plane builds on that. 'Builds' is exactly the right word here, because just when you thought Flying Lotus was done constructing the song through a build-up, he throws another awesome layer over it. And another. And another, until it finally closes and leads to a track that brings all the tension that this album has right back. Though not much sense can be made out of this track, it's super infectious and pairs its danceability with a beauty unmatched. The second half serves is even so nice, it demands a reaction that actually could best be described as ugly: German Haircut. Excellent musicianship makes this into a very interesting interlude. Then, the most powerful and beautiful track on the album: Recoiled. A banger, but one you probably haven't ever seen prettier (don't think so? wait 'til the second part of the track; gets me every time). After, more funky-ness with the odd Dance of the Psuedo Nymph, a track that is best described by its title. Once more does FlyLo show what kind of epic we're talking here: Drips is filled with strings and harp. We're talking VERY epic. Table Tennis becomes one smooth goodbye for the album before it blasts into its grand opus: Galaxi In Janaki. Everything that's in this album is perfectly mixed into one single track. You'd think this is damn near impossible with the range of genres and emotions that come to pass on this album, and genres and emotions that usually don't even mix that well at that. Here it is presented pretty cohesively though, he isn't just taking bits of other genres, but he gets to the bottom of them in as little time as he can. King Gizzard's essential album right here, folks. There's no doubt that this is quite possibly their magnum opus. Aside from all the hype surrounding this album, Nonagon Infinity is a wonderful infinitely looping collection of nine wonderful tunes that showcase some of the best of the Aussie psych rockers' songwriting, instrumentally and lyrically.

This album kicks off with the wonderful iconic tune "Robot Stop," and I'm sure everyone knows why it's so damn awesome. It's got the catchy riffs and lyrics to make a good first impression for any first-time listeners. With all this being said, it's pretty easy to see how, with such masterful consistency, Nonagon Infinity is often considered to be the best King Gizzard & the Lizard Wizard album. Through the interconnectedness of the lyrics and musical motifs of the songs on here, Nonagon presents a neatly arranged spiderweb of music that is genuinely worth being trapped in.

When it was released many of my friends hailed it as a classic, and I was kind of bewildered by it. 1995 shoegaze from Argentina already sounds intriguing, right? Well if not, this will hopefully grab your attention. Dormitorio really kick things off on a HOLY SHIT note. The opening track Domingo Ginsu is one of those shoegaze songs where the vocals are gorgeous, the bassline melds perfectly with its’ surroundings and every new chord feels better than the last. It is pure shimmering bliss and I never want it to end. It was to my surprise then, that what follows is a downtempo piece where the primary focus is not layers of electric guitars but instead a groovy bassline… for almost 12 minutes. Corazón TV thus feels more like a weird mix of slowcore and dub. It also hints at how typical layered shoegaze noisiness is used for a good half of the album- quite sparingly outside of choruses. On a purely noise-based spectrum, most of this is quite close to Eccsame the Photon Band except even slower-moving and the drum sound isn’t as big. That might sound like a pretty specific niche, but in a genre full of imitators it’s a very welcome one.

When there are no vocals to build up to a chorus it produces a pretty interesting result as well. Abolico feels like the tail-end of a superbly executed fuzzed-out transition only extra spaced-out both in length and aesthetic. Speaking of the spacey, the additional electronic sounds sprinkled throughout this album sound like they weren’t sourced from either a keyboard or a synthesizer but a control panel in the cockpit of a cartoon-like spaceship. Such sounds aren’t used constantly but when they are they work as a secret weapon to Dormitorio’s overall sense of sparkle.

Three of the heaviest tracks appear on the second half. Su jardin is the most down-tuned and is almost entirely comprised of deep, dark-red powerchords, while Parpatene is enveloped in echoed delay with some kind of screechy-filter applied to the additional vocal track. The closing track Divide y reinarás is actually two songs, with the first one exploring similar territory to Parpatene. After a brief silence the second kicks in ending the album on its’ most upbeat note before morphing into this rather bizarre fluttering water sound.


The upbeat note Dormitorio ends on is an outlier for what is an album of slow moving beauty. At a whopping 72 minutes in total there are multiple instances where perhaps some of the tracks could have been cut down in length. Another way to look at it though, is that this duration provides a plethora of cool sounds to discover AND it successfully fulfills a pretty unique niche: by and large these are shoegazey, Spanish lullabies to play in zero gravity. If you don’t have 72 minutes to spare, then please do at least listen to the first track because, again, HOLY SHIT.

How to describe Friko to someone who hasn't listened to them before? Hmm. They're an indie rock band that feel like successors to the indie greats of the 1990s and 2000s such as Modest Mouse and Arcade Fire. They wear their influences on their sleeve, but they are varied enough that it's hard to pin them down. Their special sauce is lead vocalist Niko Kapetan, whose voice I can best describe as a zoomer take on the vocal affectations of Conor Oberst or Isaac Brock.

When I heard their 2023 debut Where We've Been, Where We Go From Here I found them to have great potential. The album had some fantastic cuts, but at the time, their faithful revival of classic indie rock wasn't quite as interesting as other modern bands with more bold approaches to this genre, such as Black Country, New Road or even Geese more recently.

But still, all the pieces for a top-tier band were there. I expected them to slowly grow and improve record to record. To my surprise, it seems they have skipped all of that and just fast-forwarded to casually dropping one of the most stunning indie rock albums I've ever heard.

Something Worth Waiting For still definitely exists within the same universe as Where We've Been. But it's an astronomical improvement in almost every way. Niko is significantly more confident and energetic this time around, taking way more risks with his vocals. He's got the range to come off as cute with the puppy yelps of “Still Around" and pulls off powerful screams on the title track. On the bare opener "Guess," he commands your attention with a gravitas of a Jeff Mangum.

This time around, while the musical influences are still clearly identifiable, they are much more colorful. The overarching theme I hear is, the alternative rock styles of Radiohead and Modest Mouse, with just a tinge of 1970s singer-songwriter color and songwriting.

The album flows incredibly well too, and it feels much more cohesive than their debut. I typically don't spend time going track-by-track, but it just feels wrong not to here.

"Guess" starts things off intimately, with just guitar and a stunning vocal performance that provides the album's mission statement. Don't make me guess between laughing or crying, optimism and cynicism, because the world is so dark that it feels wrong to choose happiness. It's the dual forces tearing at you from either side, and Niko sings with an incredibly honest desperation.

They reward you for sitting through the intensity of the opener with the euphoric, bouncy pop of "Still Around." I hear a little bit of the Killers here and a little bit of britpop there, with lyrics that optimistically search for a silver lining. It has a really sticky chorus and a sing-along bridge and coda that is sure to go HARD in live performances.

I find "Choo Choo" to be the centerpiece of the album, and perhaps its best vertical slice. Niko's lyricism has always expressed a restlessness that I feel is best exemplified here. Being stuck in one place, and wanting run away. To take the train as far as it can go. The "choo choo" refrain feels mystical, using that childish wordplay as a magical incantation. I ESPECIALLY love the incredibly tight, elastic performance. The band plays with tempo, culminating in a massive finale where I can almost visually see the out-of-control speeding train in its increasing tempo and noisy, chaotic guitars.

"Alice" gives you a respite from the loud rock of the previous two tracks with a heartfelt pop rock ballad. Friko did this same thing with the sequencing on their first album, and these more restrained moments were the least interesting parts of that album. But this track bucks that trend with an excellent instrumental build that crescendoes into a spectacular payoff. They are just doing it with pianos and na-na-nahs instead of loud guitars and screams. The Alice in Wonderland imagery creates an really enchanted vibe, too.

"Certainty" then sees Friko indulge themselves with another ballad, this one being more of an oddball baroque chamber pop tune with a lush orchestral arrangement. Think "She's Leaving Home" by the Beatles. It feels like an odd turn in the sequencing of the record, but it's performed so well that it's hard to fault it too much, and it serves well as an intermission at the center of the album.

"Hot Air Balloon" resets things with a more urgent cut of jangle pop. We return to the themes of restlessness and a desire for escape, seeing a hot air balloon as the escape from the troubles of the world. It's got a very catchy sing-along chorus that I’m sure will make this a popular favorite.

"Seven Degrees" throws you another curveball, starting with only vocals and acoustic guitar. But soon enough, more vocals are layered in, then the band comes in to give you a satisfying finish. This is a heartfelt singalong playing on the theme of six-degrees of separation, adding just one more degree representing the one person that they can't find. Their severed soul. The poppy melody really conceals the devastation in its lyrics, desperately holding on to hope that you can find that one person, that one thing to make you happy.

The climax of the album comes in "Something Worth Waiting For," which again starts with just acoustic guitar and vocals, and has two notable crescendoes. It first blows up into a driving, cacophonous guitar solo before pulling back and starting its build a second time. But the second time, it instead breaks into an even louder section, so crushingly loud and abrasive that it approaches noise rock. It’s great. Lyrically, it calls back to multiple themes from their first album, from songs as “Statues” and “Where We’ve Been.”

"Dear Bicycle" ends things on a nocturnal slowcore ballad. It may come off as surprising choice on initial listen, but thematically this is such a great way to end. It’s like the afterglow after the gritty crescendo of the previous track. They create a beautiful, gentle texture that eases you out of the album. The song uses the imagery of an old, rusted bicycle hiding in the back of a shed as a stand-in for a person, urging for renewal and rebirth. The final words are impactful as well. Friko have now spent two album cycles ruminating on spatial themes, “where we are” and “where we’ve been”. But “Dear Bicycle” points forward, begging you not to forget “where you’ve yet to go” as well. It’s an incredibly hopeful, satisfying, full-circle ending.

Tons of bands try to pull off this sound. But Friko is indeed on another level. The transcendent songwriting and lyricism, the raw and energetic performances, it all is so excellent. They don’t hide their influences, sure, but they certainly do channel the best things about the bands of the past that inspire their music. If this is what Friko can achieve this early into their career, I’m excited for where they’ll end up next.
