RichardWiggins Jun 08 2018
Vote downBookmarkVote up
Minimalist maximalism.
Futuristic techno groans and pulsating beats whirl and whip into a release which is both slow and fast, full on but ambient. There’s not much going on at any one time - but every bit of energy is drawn out of every note to the point where Daniel Avery’s minimalist approach becomes maximalist.

Some milder cuts on the back end stop it from being an out and out success, but there’s no doubt that a large chunk of this LP is truly inspired and a must listen.

diction May 11 2018
Vote downBookmarkVote up
Radically different from Drone Logic, this time around Daniel Avery made a more cohesive record that mostly sticks with the same sound all around. Ambient Techno, but not quite as Ambient as Gas or Deepchord, but still something I would put in the listen for a chill experience pile. The album itself is pretty much flawless, every beat is really well coordinated and keeps the vibe going throughout. Quite the enjoyable release and I think it's a proof that Daniel Avery has a good career ahead of him, showcasing here that he can easily do another sound just as well. Good stuff.

hyperi0n May 01 2018
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
As the Nostalgia Wars™ continue, it seems like now were gonna go and pillage Aphex Twin's back catalogue (as if that hasn't already been done enough). In fact, this album "borrows" so heavily from the SAW Aphex period that you can even hear the shitty, distorted reverb coming from a Quadraverb (which is all over both SAW albums).

Derivatives aside, there are actually some nice ambient tracks here, even if they don't really go anywhere. The techno tracks are boring and pointless, but it's techno in 2018, so that comes with the territory.

The best track on here is probably Projector, which has some cool sound design and interesting chord progressions. Other than that though, everything else is mediocre and listless.

This would be a solid "average" album, though, if it wasn't for the 303. It's 2018 people, you can buy sweatshop analog synths for less than a dinner date (not that any of you know how much that costs). Find another synth for fucks sake, it's been nearly 40 years.

Album of the year thus far? I’m yet to encounter any other record out there that builds an atmosphere quite as well as Song For Alpha does, that’s for sure. natespirateradio Apr 09 2018
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Avery is my kind of techno producer, as he turns his back on the club to make a hazy, psychedelic techno record. The dance floor influences are still there, but they’re saturated with ambient, 90s Warp, and downtempo. “Stereo L” sets a tone by winding a 303 over an atypical bass beat, and is followed by “Projector”, a fun listen that is both too fast and too slow to dance to, but just right to write music reviews to. “Sensation” actually uses a four to the floor and would probably be a banger if it weren’t for tastefully restrained sound design. “Clear” is psychedelic ambience and reverb-laden synth over sixteenth-note clipped percussion. Even centerpiece “Diminuendo,” which is the most club-ready of the tracks, is also very much not, with its descent into ambience too long and sharp before it’s awesome (spoiler-alert) huge beat drop. The latter part of the record is quieter like the beginning, with the single “Slow Fade” carrying to the locked-groove finale at the end of “Quick Eternity.”
